# Chapter 01: Introduction

## 1.1 What Problem Does SSH Actually Solve?

Before any protocol detail, it's worth being precise about the problem SSH exists to solve, because every design decision in the chapters that follow is a direct answer to this problem, not an arbitrary engineering choice.

Before SSH, remote administration relied on protocols such as **Telnet** (RFC 854, 1983), **rlogin**, and **rsh** (the BSD "r-commands," documented informally alongside 4.2BSD, 1983). These protocols share a property that, in retrospect, is almost hard to believe was ever the operational norm: every byte — including the password typed at the login prompt — is transmitted as plaintext. No cryptographic transformation is applied at any point. Anyone positioned anywhere along the network path between client and host — a shared Ethernet segment, a compromised switch, a rogue Wi-Fi access point, a malicious router — could recover credentials with nothing more than a passive packet capture. There was no cryptography to defeat, because there was none to begin with.

SSH was designed by Tatu Ylönen at Helsinki University of Technology in 1995, directly in response to a password-sniffing incident on his university network that compromised a large number of accounts via exactly this weakness. The original implementation (later retroactively called SSH-1) was released as free software and adopted rapidly. In 1996, Ylönen and collaborators published a redesigned protocol, SSH-2, which was not a patch on SSH-1 but a ground-up architectural rewrite. SSH-2 was standardized by the IETF "secsh" working group and published as RFC 4251 through RFC 4254 in January 2006 — roughly a decade after the informal SSH-2 draft first circulated, reflecting the length of the standardization process rather than the maturity timeline of the protocol itself.

SSH's founding design goal can be stated as three separable security properties that must all hold simultaneously:

1. **Confidentiality** — no party observing the network path should be able to recover the plaintext content of the session.
2. **Integrity** — no party should be able to modify traffic in transit without the modification being detected and the connection terminated.
3. **Authentication** — both endpoints need a cryptographically sound way to establish who they are communicating with: the server proves its identity to the client, and (separately) the client proves its identity to the server.

These are not three facets of one property; they are three independent properties that can, in principle, fail independently of one another. A channel can be confidential but not authenticated (an attacker can't read your data but might still be a man-in-the-middle relaying it). A channel can be authenticated but not confidential (you know exactly who you're talking to, but anyone can read the conversation). This independence is precisely why SSH is not built as one monolithic protocol — it is the direct motivation for the three-layer architecture (transport, authentication, connection) that Chapter 02 covers in full.

## 1.2 SSH-1 vs. SSH-2: Why This Series Only Covers SSH-2

SSH-1 and SSH-2 are incompatible major versions at the wire-protocol level — not merely different feature sets of the same design. This distinction is not academic; SSH-1 has a documented structural weakness, independent of implementation bugs, that is worth understanding briefly because it illustrates *why* SSH-2's architecture looks the way it does.

SSH-1 used a single integrity mechanism based on a 32-bit CRC checksum layered under encryption in CBC mode. Researchers (notably the CORE SDI team, publishing what became known as the "SSH insertion attack" in 1998) demonstrated that this construction allowed an active network attacker to insert chosen plaintext into an SSH-1 session without invalidating the CRC — the checksum was not a cryptographically sound message authentication code, and its interaction with CBC-mode block cipher chaining created an exploitable structural gap. This was not a bug in one particular implementation; it was a flaw in what the protocol itself specified as "integrity protection."

SSH-2's response was to replace the ad hoc CRC with a proper, cryptographically-keyed **MAC** (message authentication code) computed with an algorithm and key explicitly negotiated and derived as part of the transport layer's key-exchange output (Section 2.3.4 in Chapter 02) — integrity became a first-class, independently-keyed cryptographic property rather than an incidental checksum. This single historical episode is a useful lens for the rest of this series: much of SSH-2's apparent complexity — six independently derived keys, a formally specified exchange hash, explicit per-message sequence numbers to prevent replay and reordering — exists because SSH-1's simpler, less rigorous design was shown to be exploitable in exactly the ways rigor would have prevented.

Every modern implementation — including OpenSSH, which this series uses throughout — defaults to SSH-2 exclusively, and recent OpenSSH releases have removed SSH-1 support from the codebase entirely rather than merely disabling it by default. For this reason, this series treats SSH-1 as a historical case study, not a protocol you should expect to encounter or need to configure around. When "SSH" appears anywhere in this series without qualification, it denotes SSH-2.

## 1.3 The Threat Model

A threat model is only useful if it precisely answers three questions: *who* is the adversary, *what capabilities* does that adversary have, and *what, specifically,* are we defending against. A security discussion that skips this formalization tends to collapse into vague reassurance ("SSH is secure") rather than a defensible engineering claim. This section is deliberately precise.

### 1.3.1 A Minimal Formal Frame: Adversary Capability Classes

Cryptographic protocol analysis conventionally distinguishes adversaries by capability, not by identity. It is useful to borrow this framing (closely related to the Dolev–Yao model used broadly in protocol verification) and apply it concretely to SSH:

- **Passive network observer** — can read every packet on the path but cannot alter, drop, delay, inject, or reorder any of them.
- **Active on-path attacker** — occupies a position on the actual network path (e.g., a compromised router or a malicious Wi-Fi access point) and can read, drop, delay, modify, or inject packets.
- **Active off-path attacker** — is not physically on the network path but can inject spoofed packets (e.g., via IP spoofing or a routing manipulation) without necessarily seeing the legitimate traffic.
- **Compromised endpoint** — has arbitrary code execution on the client or the server itself, prior to or independent of any SSH session.

Each of SSH's guarantees below should be read as a claim about which of these adversary classes it holds against — and, just as importantly, which ones it does *not* hold against.

### 1.3.2 What SSH Protects Against

**A passive network observer.** SSH's transport-layer encryption (Chapter 02, Section 2.3) is specifically designed so that a passive observer, even one capturing 100% of the traffic between client and server, learns nothing about the plaintext content of the session. This is a guarantee against the passive observer class in the frame above. It is worth being exact about what remains visible even to a purely passive observer: connection timing, the fact that a connection to a particular host and port exists, total bytes transferred in each direction, and — because SSH does not pad interactive traffic to a fixed size by default — the approximate size and timing of individual keystroke packets in an interactive session. We return to this last point below, because it is a real and researched limitation, not a hypothetical one.

**An active on-path attacker attempting impersonation.** SSH's host key verification (Chapter 02, Section 2.3.5) is designed to let the client detect if it is talking to an impostor rather than the intended server. This guarantee is conditional, not unconditional — it holds only if the client has some prior, trustworthy knowledge of the legitimate server's host key to compare against. We formalize exactly how conditional this is in Section 1.3.3 below, because it is the single most commonly misunderstood aspect of SSH's security model.

**Credential exposure via network capture.** Because authentication occurs entirely inside the already-encrypted transport channel (Chapter 02, Section 2.2, step 4), neither a passive observer nor most active on-path positions can recover a password or private-key material by observing traffic — a direct, deliberate contrast with Telnet's plaintext credential exchange.

**Tampering in transit.** The per-packet MAC (Chapter 02, Section 2.3.4) provides cryptographic integrity: any active on-path attacker who modifies even a single bit of a packet in transit causes MAC verification to fail on the receiving end, and the connection is torn down rather than silently delivering corrupted or attacker-controlled data. Combined with explicit per-packet sequence numbers included in the MAC computation, this also defeats packet replay and reordering attacks — precisely the class of attack that defeated SSH-1's CRC-based approach.

**Forward secrecy.** Because SSH-2's key exchange uses ephemeral Diffie-Hellman (or its elliptic-curve variant, Section 2.3.2), the symmetric session keys are derived from key material generated fresh for that session and discarded afterward — never from a long-term static key. The consequence: even if an attacker later compromises the server's long-term host private key, past recorded sessions cannot be decrypted retroactively, because the session keys were never derivable from the long-term key alone. This property, forward secrecy, is not incidental — it is a direct, load-bearing consequence of the specific key-exchange design choice covered in Chapter 02, and it's worth understanding as a design decision rather than a side effect.

### 1.3.3 What SSH Does *Not* Protect Against

This list is at least as important as the previous one. Nearly every real-world SSH security failure traces back to an operator implicitly assuming a guarantee that was never actually part of the model.

**A compromised endpoint.** If either the client machine or the server itself has been compromised — malware, a keylogger, a kernel-level rootkit, a supply-chain-compromised binary — SSH's cryptography is not merely weakened, it is entirely beside the point. SSH secures the channel *between* two points; it makes no claim whatsoever about the trustworthiness of either point. This is precisely the "compromised endpoint" adversary class from Section 1.3.1, and no amount of transport-layer or authentication-layer rigor addresses it. This is why Chapter 06 treats host hardening as a wholly separate discipline: SSH configuration is necessary but never sufficient for the security of a system.

**A weak, reused, or guessable password**, when password authentication is enabled at all. SSH will faithfully, correctly, and securely transport a weak password to the server, encrypted end-to-end the entire way — encryption of the channel says precisely nothing about the entropy or strength of what is placed inside it. Chapter 03 develops this into a formal argument for why public-key authentication is architecturally superior, not merely more convenient: the object being verified (a signature over the session identifier) has cryptographic properties a human-memorable password structurally cannot have.

**Naive first-contact trust — the Trust On First Use (TOFU) gap.** This deserves the most careful treatment of any item on this list, because it is the most consequential and the most commonly misunderstood. Host key verification (Section 1.3.2 above) only detects impersonation if the client already possesses a trustworthy reference copy of the server's legitimate host key. On the *first* connection to a given host, no such reference exists yet — the client has only what the server itself just sent during key exchange. SSH's default behavior in this situation is what the security literature calls a "leap of faith": it presents the offered key's fingerprint to the user, asks for a one-time confirmation, and if confirmed, stores that key in `known_hosts` for all future comparisons. If an active on-path attacker is positioned during precisely this first connection, they can present their own key, the user confirms it without independent verification, and every guarantee described in Section 1.3.2 now protects a channel to the attacker, not the intended server — silently and with no further warning on subsequent connections. This is not a bug or an oversight in the protocol; it is an explicit, acknowledged design tradeoff, and Chapter 03 covers the mechanisms available to close this gap (out-of-band fingerprint verification, SSHFP DNS records, and certificate-based host authentication).

**Authorization decisions.** SSH authenticates *identity* — proving that you are the holder of a given credential. It makes no decision, by itself, about what that identity is subsequently permitted to do. That is governed by an entirely separate layer: the operating system's own file and process permission model, `sudo` policy, and — as Chapter 04 and Chapter 06 cover — SSH-level restrictions such as forced commands and per-key authorization constraints in `authorized_keys`. Conflating authentication with authorization is one of the most common sources of real-world misconfiguration, and this series treats them as strictly separate concerns throughout.

**Traffic metadata and side channels.** As noted in Section 1.3.2, SSH's confidentiality guarantee covers content, not the existence, approximate volume, or timing of a session. This is not merely a theoretical caveat: published research on interactive SSH sessions has demonstrated that because each keystroke in an interactive terminal session is typically transmitted as its own small packet in real time (rather than buffered and sent in batches), the *inter-keystroke timing* pattern visible even in fully encrypted traffic leaks statistically exploitable information — including, in some studied conditions, information correlated with password length and, via keystroke-timing biometrics, partial information about the keystrokes themselves. This is a genuine, published limitation of interactive terminal protocols generally (not unique to SSH, and not fixed by any SSH configuration option), and it is a useful concrete illustration of why "the channel is encrypted" and "no information is leaked" are not the same claim.

## 1.4 A Note on Assumed Environment

Every command, default behavior, and configuration example throughout this series assumes **OpenSSH 9.x**, protocol version 2 exclusively. This assumption is not cosmetic — it has concrete technical consequences. OpenSSH 9.x has removed or changed several defaults present in earlier major versions: the legacy SCP wire protocol is no longer the default file-transfer mechanism (an SFTP-based transfer path is used by default instead, specifically to avoid a class of parsing ambiguities in the original SCP protocol's handling of filenames); several key-exchange and cipher algorithms that were enabled by default in older releases are now either disabled by default or require explicit administrator opt-in, reflecting cryptographic best-practice guidance that has evolved since those defaults were first set.

Where a later chapter's specifics — particularly default algorithm ordering, flag behavior, or file-format details — are version-sensitive, that chapter will state the version dependency explicitly rather than leave it as an unstated assumption. If you are administering an older OpenSSH release or a non-OpenSSH implementation (e.g., Dropbear, or a proprietary vendor SSH stack), treat every concrete default mentioned in this series as something to verify against your specific version's documentation, even where the underlying protocol mechanism (governed by the RFCs, not by any one implementation) remains identical.

## 1.5 How to Read This Series

Two methodological commitments are established here and held without exception through every subsequent chapter.

**Primary sources over secondhand claims.** Wherever a chapter asserts a fact about protocol behavior, that assertion is grounded in RFC 4251–4254, the OpenSSH source tree, or OpenSSH's own man pages — not in the layer of accumulated blog-post and forum folklore that circulates around SSH, a meaningful fraction of which is outdated, imprecise, or was never correct to begin with. Where a claim rests on external research (as in the keystroke-timing side-channel discussion in Section 1.3.3), that is flagged explicitly as external research rather than presented as protocol specification.

**Observation over assertion.** Wherever the underlying behavior can be directly observed — with `ssh -vvv`, `sshd -ddd`, `ssh -Q`, `ssh-keyscan`, or a raw packet capture of the plaintext portions of a handshake — this series shows the exact command and the expected output, rather than asking you to accept a claim on faith. Chapter 02 exercises this discipline extensively, walking an entire handshake end to end against real tool output; treat that chapter's approach as the template for how every subsequent claim in this series can, in principle, be independently verified on your own systems.

With the problem precisely defined and the threat model made explicit — including, deliberately, its boundaries — the next question is mechanical: *how does SSH actually deliver the three properties named in Section 1.1?* Chapter 02 answers this by walking through the three-layer architecture — transport, user authentication, and connection — from the first plaintext byte exchanged on the wire through to a fully multiplexed, authenticated session, grounding every mechanism introduced here (host key verification, forward secrecy, per-packet MACs) in the specific protocol machinery that produces it.
