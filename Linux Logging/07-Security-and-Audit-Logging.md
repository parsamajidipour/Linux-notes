# Security and Audit Logging

Every mechanism covered so far in this series — `journald`, `rsyslog`, the kernel ring buffer, application-level logging — shares a common underlying purpose: recording what happened, for operational visibility and troubleshooting. This chapter covers a mechanism with a genuinely different design intent: the Linux **audit subsystem**, built specifically to answer a narrower, more security-focused question — not merely "what happened," but "who did this specific, security-relevant thing, precisely, in a form that can stand up as reliable forensic and compliance evidence." Understanding why this requires a structurally distinct mechanism, rather than simply being "very detailed" application or service logging, is this chapter's central thread.

---

## 1. Why Audit Logging Needs to Be Structurally Different

### 1.1 The Core Distinction: Operational Visibility Versus Accountability

Every logging mechanism covered in Chapters 2 through 6 is optimized, in one way or another, for **operational visibility** — helping an administrator understand system behavior, diagnose failures, and monitor health. The audit subsystem is optimized for a different, narrower goal: **accountability** — establishing, with a high degree of confidence and in a form suitable for later scrutiny, exactly which identity performed exactly which security-relevant action, at exactly what time, against exactly what object. This distinction is worth holding precisely because it explains nearly every structural difference covered in the rest of this chapter: general-purpose logging is written by the source that generated the event (an application choosing what to log, and how), while audit logging is generated by **kernel-level interception of specific system calls**, entirely independent of whether the process performing the action has any logging awareness, or even any interest in being logged, at all.

### 1.2 Why Application-Level Logging Cannot Substitute for Audit Logging

This is worth stating precisely, because it directly explains why this chapter's subject matter can't simply be folded into Chapter 6's application-logging material. An application, however well-instrumented, can only log what it *chooses* to log, using its own internal logic — meaning application-level logging is fundamentally dependent on the honesty and thoroughness of the specific application's own code. A compromised or maliciously modified application can simply choose not to log its own malicious actions, or can log false information, and application-level logging provides no independent means of detecting this at all. Audit logging, by contrast, operates by having the **kernel itself** intercept and record specific system calls at the point they're actually made — a mechanism a compromised userspace application cannot bypass merely by choosing not to call some logging function, since the recording happens independent of and beneath any application's own cooperation, at the exact boundary where the kernel's own permission-checking machinery (the subject of the permissions series this logging series follows) is already, necessarily, involved.

### 1.3 Compliance and Forensic Context

Directly connecting to Chapter 4, Section 3.1's mention of compliance-driven retention requirements: audit logging is very often the specific category of log data regulatory and compliance frameworks have in mind when they impose minimum retention or specific event-coverage requirements — file access to sensitive data, authentication attempts (successful and failed alike), privilege escalation events, and configuration changes to security-relevant system files are common, recurring categories of event that compliance frameworks across many industries and jurisdictions specifically require reliable, tamper-resistant tracking of, precisely because of the accountability property Section 1.1 identified — a compliance auditor's question is rarely "what happened, roughly" but specifically "who did this, and can you demonstrate it reliably."

## 2. The Audit Subsystem's Architecture

### 2.1 Kernel-Level Hooks and auditd

The audit subsystem consists of two cooperating parts, worth distinguishing precisely. The **kernel audit framework** itself contains hooks placed at specific points in the kernel's own system-call handling code — points that, when a matching audit rule is active (Section 3), generate a raw audit event describing exactly what occurred. **`auditd`**, the userspace audit daemon, is responsible for receiving these kernel-generated events (via a dedicated netlink socket, a kernel-to-userspace communication mechanism distinct from the syslog socket Chapter 3 covered) and writing them to persistent storage, typically `/var/log/audit/audit.log`.

This two-part architecture directly parallels, in spirit, the ring-buffer-versus-journald relationship Chapter 5 established for kernel boot messages — a kernel-level mechanism generating the actual event data, paired with a separate userspace component responsible for durable storage and later querying — but worth being precise that the audit subsystem's kernel-to-userspace transport (the dedicated netlink socket) and its storage format are **entirely separate** from journald's own mechanisms, not a variant or extension of them, even though, as Section 2.3 covers, some integration and cross-referencing between the two systems does exist in practice.

### 2.2 Why a Dedicated Daemon Rather Than Journald Directly

This is worth explaining precisely, since a reasonable question is why the audit subsystem doesn't simply feed directly into journald's existing structured-storage infrastructure, given that Chapter 2 already established journald as a general-purpose unification point for exactly this kind of structured event data. The direct answer connects to Section 1.1's accountability framing: audit logging has specific, independent reliability and tamper-resistance requirements — including the ability to be configured to **halt the entire system** rather than silently continue operating if audit logging itself fails (Section 4.3) — a behavior that would be a genuinely inappropriate default for general-purpose operational logging (nobody wants a routine `journald` hiccup to crash the entire system), but is precisely the correct, deliberate behavior for a subsystem whose entire purpose is providing reliable accountability guarantees for regulatory and forensic contexts, where an undetected gap in the audit trail can itself constitute a serious compliance and security failure in its own right.

### 2.3 The Existing Integration Point

Despite being architecturally separate, worth noting the genuine, practical integration that does exist: audit events can be, and commonly are, additionally routed into the journal as well (audit data appearing alongside other journal content, tagged with audit-specific fields), giving administrators the option of using `journalctl`'s query interface (Chapter 2, Section 5) as one additional, convenient lens onto audit data, without this secondary availability in any way replacing `auditd`'s own dedicated, purpose-built storage and query tooling (Section 5) as the authoritative, compliance-grade audit record.

## 3. Audit Rules: Defining What Gets Recorded

### 3.1 The Rule-Based Model

Unlike the mechanisms covered in earlier chapters, which largely capture whatever a source chooses to emit (Chapter 6's stdout/stderr model) or everything the kernel generates unconditionally (Chapter 5's ring buffer), the audit subsystem operates on an explicit, administrator-defined **rule set** — nothing is audited by default beyond a small baseline, and meaningful audit coverage requires deliberately configuring rules describing exactly which system calls, against which files, by which users, should generate audit events. This is worth understanding as a deliberate design choice directly following from Section 1.1's accountability framing: unconditional, blanket auditing of every single system call on a busy system would generate an overwhelming, largely useless volume of data, while a precisely scoped rule set produces a signal-rich, genuinely reviewable record focused specifically on the security-relevant events an administrator or compliance framework has determined actually matter.

### 3.2 File Watch Rules

```
auditctl -w /etc/shadow -p wa -k shadow-changes
auditctl -w /etc/passwd -p wa -k passwd-changes
```

Reading this syntax precisely: `-w` specifies the watched path; `-p wa` specifies which access types trigger the rule (`w` for write, `a` for attribute change — directly connecting to the permissions series' own material on what "attribute change" means at the inode level); `-k` attaches an arbitrary, administrator-chosen search key, used later (Section 5.2) to efficiently retrieve exactly the events this specific rule generated, without needing to reconstruct the original rule's matching criteria from scratch at query time.

This example is worth connecting directly back to the permissions series' Chapter 2 material: `/etc/shadow`, established there as the file holding password hashes and requiring the strictest access restriction of any standard system file, is precisely the kind of file where audit logging's accountability guarantee — knowing definitively *who* accessed or modified it, not merely *that* the file's permissions technically prevented unauthorized access — provides genuine, independent security value beyond what permission enforcement alone offers; permission bits prevent unauthorized access, while audit rules provide a durable record of *authorized* access as well, which matters directly for detecting misuse of legitimately held privilege, a category of concern permission enforcement alone structurally cannot address at all.

### 3.3 System-Call Rules

Beyond watching specific files, audit rules can target specific system calls directly, optionally filtered by additional criteria:

```
auditctl -a always,exit -F arch=b64 -S execve -F auid>=1000 -F auid!=4294967295 -k user-executions
```

This rule — worth reading through carefully since it's considerably denser than the file-watch syntax — records every successful `execve()` (program execution) system call, on 64-bit architecture, made by any process whose **audit UID** (`auid`, Section 3.4) is 1000 or greater (conventionally, per the permissions series' Chapter 2 material on UID ranges, a regular human user rather than a system account) and is not the specific sentinel value `4294967295` (representing "no audit UID has been set at all," a case Section 3.4 explains). This is a genuinely common, real-world pattern — comprehensive tracking of every program a human user actually executes — directly useful for detecting anomalous or unauthorized command execution in a security-monitoring context.

### 3.4 The Audit UID: A Distinct Identity Concept

This is worth its own dedicated explanation because it's a genuinely important, easily-confused addition to the identity model the permissions series built across its own Chapter 2. The **audit UID** (`auid`, also called the "login UID") is a *separate* credential field, distinct from the real, effective, saved, and filesystem UIDs the permissions series covered in full — and critically, **it is set once, at initial login, and does not change even if the process subsequently changes its effective or real UID** through `setuid()`, `su`, `sudo`, or any other mechanism the permissions series' Chapter 2 detailed.

This immutability is precisely the point, and it directly closes a genuine accountability gap the ordinary UID model alone cannot address: if a user logs in as `alice` and subsequently uses `sudo` to perform an action as root, the process's *effective* UID at the moment of that action is `0` — but its *audit UID* remains `1005` (alice's own UID), preserving, permanently and unchangeably for the life of that specific login session, the answer to "which human being originally authenticated and is ultimately accountable for this entire chain of actions," regardless of how many subsequent privilege transitions occurred along the way. This is precisely the mechanism that makes an audit trail like Section 3.3's example genuinely useful for accountability purposes even in environments with heavy, legitimate `sudo` usage — without the audit UID's specific immutability property, tracing a root-level action back to the specific human who ultimately initiated it, through however many `sudo` hops occurred, would be considerably harder or, in some cases, impossible to reconstruct reliably after the fact.

## 4. Audit Log Integrity and Failure Behavior

### 4.1 Configuration Immutability

The audit subsystem supports a specific, deliberately drastic configuration mode:

```
-e 2
```

Set via `auditctl -e 2` or the equivalent persistent rule-file directive, this makes the **audit configuration itself immutable for the remainder of the current boot session** — no further rule changes can be made at all, by anyone, including root, until the next reboot. This is worth understanding as a direct, deliberate application of the permissions series' own Chapter 8 immutable-flag concept (Section 1.5 of that chapter), but applied here specifically to audit *configuration* rather than to a specific file's content — the goal being to prevent an attacker who has achieved root-level access from simply disabling or narrowing audit rules to cover their subsequent tracks, precisely the kind of confused-deputy-adjacent, "compromised privileged access undermining the very accountability mechanism meant to detect it" scenario Chapter 9 of the permissions series discussed in the DAC-versus-MAC context.

### 4.2 Disk-Full Behavior: A Deliberately Different Default Philosophy

This is worth contrasting directly and explicitly against Chapter 4's entire disk-exhaustion discussion for general-purpose logging, because the audit subsystem's philosophy here is a deliberate, principled inversion of that earlier material's assumptions:

```
[Options]
disk_full_action = SUSPEND
disk_error_action = SUSPEND
```

Chapter 4 treated disk exhaustion purely as a failure mode to be prevented through careful rotation and retention configuration (Chapter 4, Section 1-4), with the implicit assumption throughout that continuing to operate, even with degraded or lost logging, is preferable to the system itself failing. The audit subsystem explicitly supports, and in genuinely high-security or strictly regulated deployments often deliberately configures, the **opposite** default: `SUSPEND` (or, more drastically, `halt`, which stops the entire system outright) rather than silently allowing audit events to be lost when storage is exhausted or otherwise fails. This directly reflects Section 1.1's accountability framing taken to its logical, deliberate conclusion: for a system where regulatory or security requirements mandate that *every* covered event genuinely must be reliably recorded, silently continuing to operate with gaps in the audit trail is considered a worse outcome than the system itself becoming unavailable — a value judgment that would be entirely inappropriate for general-purpose operational logging (per Chapter 4's own framing), but is a legitimate, deliberate, and in many regulated industries actually mandated choice specifically for audit logging.

### 4.3 Why This Distinction Matters for Deployment Decisions

Worth stating explicitly as a practical takeaway: an administrator configuring audit logging needs to make a genuinely different set of trade-off decisions than the ones Chapter 4 walked through for general logging — the "more retention is generally better, balanced against disk cost" framing from Chapter 4, Section 3.1 needs to be supplemented, specifically for audit data, with an explicit decision about failure behavior that has real operational consequences (a `SUSPEND` or `halt` configuration genuinely can, and by design sometimes will, render a system unavailable rather than silently lose audit coverage) — a decision that should be made deliberately, with the specific regulatory or security context in mind, rather than left at whatever default a specific distribution happens to ship with.

## 5. Querying Audit Data

### 5.1 ausearch: The Purpose-Built Query Tool

Directly paralleling `journalctl`'s role for the journal (Chapter 2, Section 5), `ausearch` is the dedicated query interface for audit log data:

```
ausearch -k shadow-changes                    # events matching a specific rule's key, per Section 3.2
ausearch -ua 1005                              # events tied to a specific audit UID, per Section 3.4
ausearch --start today --end now               # time-bounded, directly analogous to journalctl --since/--until
ausearch -m EXECVE                              # events of a specific audit message type
```

The `-k` key-based lookup deserves particular emphasis, since it's precisely the payoff of Section 3.2's `-k` rule-configuration flag — rather than needing to reconstruct or guess at a rule's original matching criteria when querying, an administrator can tag rules meaningfully at configuration time and retrieve exactly the relevant events later using that same, memorable tag, a deliberate ease-of-use mechanism directly built into the rule-definition syntax itself.

### 5.2 aureport: Summarized, Aggregate Views

Where `ausearch` retrieves individual matching events, `aureport` produces summarized, aggregate statistics across the audit log — a genuinely different, complementary query mode worth knowing about specifically for the kind of high-level review a compliance audit or periodic security review actually needs, rather than sifting through individual raw events one at a time:

```
aureport -au              # summary of authentication events
aureport -f                # summary of file-access events
aureport -x                # summary of execve (execution) events
```

This summarized view is worth understanding as directly analogous, in spirit, to the permissions series' own Chapter 9 baseline-comparison auditing technique (the SUID/SGID inventory, run periodically and compared over time) — `aureport`'s aggregate summaries are similarly well-suited to periodic review, surfacing overall patterns and anomalies (an unusual spike in failed authentication attempts, for instance) that individual event-by-event inspection via `ausearch` alone would be considerably more tedious to notice.

## 6. Practical Baseline: A Minimal, Defensible Rule Set

This closing, practical section offers a concrete starting point, directly connecting the permissions series' own security concerns to audit rule configuration, worth treating as a reasonable baseline rather than an exhaustive, one-size-fits-all prescription, since actual, appropriate rule coverage genuinely depends on a specific deployment's own regulatory and threat-model context.

```
# Track modification of core identity files, directly per the permissions series' own Chapter 2 material
auditctl -w /etc/passwd -p wa -k identity-changes
auditctl -w /etc/shadow -p wa -k identity-changes
auditctl -w /etc/group -p wa -k identity-changes
auditctl -w /etc/sudoers -p wa -k privilege-changes

# Track use of privilege-elevation mechanisms, directly per the permissions series' Chapter 6 material
auditctl -w /usr/bin/sudo -p x -k privilege-use
auditctl -a always,exit -F arch=b64 -S setuid,setgid -F auid>=1000 -k privilege-transitions

# Track modification of the audit configuration itself, per Section 4.1's tamper-resistance concern
auditctl -w /etc/audit/audit.rules -p wa -k audit-config-changes
```

Each of these three groupings directly maps onto a specific concern this series (and its predecessor) has already established the *why* for: the first tracks exactly the identity-database files the permissions series' Chapter 2 identified as foundational and sensitive; the second tracks exactly the privilege-elevation mechanisms the permissions series' Chapter 6 covered in mechanism-level depth (SUID, and the `setuid()`/`setgid()` calls that underlie both SUID's own operation and legitimate privilege-dropping sequences alike); the third tracks changes to the audit system's own configuration, directly addressing Section 4.1's tamper-resistance concern by ensuring that even an attempt to weaken audit coverage is, itself, an event the (still-active, until successfully modified) prior configuration would capture.

## 7. Common Misconceptions Worth Retiring Now

- **"Audit logging is just very detailed application logging."** It's architecturally distinct — kernel-level system-call interception, independent of any application's own cooperation or honesty, specifically because application-level logging alone cannot provide reliable accountability against a compromised or malicious application.
- **"journalctl is a complete substitute for ausearch/aureport."** Audit data may be additionally visible through the journal as a convenience, but `auditd`'s own dedicated storage and query tooling remains the authoritative, purpose-built source for compliance and forensic purposes, with capabilities (key-based lookup, aggregate reporting) journalctl's general-purpose interface doesn't replicate.
- **"A user's UID always reflects who is accountable for an action, exactly as covered in the permissions series."** The audit UID (`auid`) is a separate, deliberately immutable credential specifically for accountability purposes, remaining fixed at the original login identity even through subsequent `sudo` or `setuid()` transitions that do change the ordinary real/effective UID fields.
- **"Losing some audit log data due to disk exhaustion is an acceptable, minor failure, the same as losing some general application log data."** The audit subsystem explicitly supports, and many regulated deployments deliberately configure, a `SUSPEND`/`halt` failure mode specifically because silent audit-trail gaps are considered a more serious failure than system unavailability, a genuinely different philosophy from Chapter 4's general-logging framing.
- **"Enabling audit logging automatically captures every security-relevant event by default."** Meaningful audit coverage requires deliberate, explicit rule configuration — nothing beyond a small baseline is captured automatically, a direct consequence of the rule-based, precisely-scoped design Section 3.1 described.
