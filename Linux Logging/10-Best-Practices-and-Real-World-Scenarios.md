# Best Practices and Real-World Scenarios

This closing chapter mirrors the structure of the permissions series' own final chapter: not new mechanism, but synthesis. Chapters 1 through 9 built a complete model of Linux logging — architecture, journald, rsyslog, rotation, kernel logs, application conventions, audit accountability, single-host troubleshooting, and fleet-wide centralization. This chapter assembles that model into a consolidated best-practices reference and a set of fully worked, realistic scenarios that exercise the entire stack.

---

## 1. A Consolidated Best-Practices Checklist

This section draws every actionable recommendation scattered across the previous nine chapters into a single, ordered reference — the direct practical payoff of the entire series.

1. **Understand which of the several coexisting mechanisms actually applies before troubleshooting or configuring anything** (Chapter 1, Section 2; Chapter 8, Section 1) — the single most consequential habit this entire series has tried to instill, since every other item on this list depends on first knowing which mechanism is actually in play.
2. **Decide deliberately between volatile and persistent journal storage** (Chapter 2, Section 2) — don't let the `/var/log/journal/` directory's mere accidental existence or absence silently determine this consequential choice; make it an explicit, documented configuration decision.
3. **Prefer trusted, underscore-prefixed journal fields for anything security- or accountability-relevant** (Chapter 2, Section 3.2) — self-reported fields can be spoofed by the source; kernel-verified fields cannot.
4. **Use explicit application-level severity rather than relying on the coarse stdout/stderr default** (Chapter 6, Section 3) wherever accurate, message-level severity genuinely matters for downstream filtering or alerting.
5. **Configure both logrotate and journald's native retention independently** (Chapter 4, Section 1.3) — they are two separate systems, and tuning only one leaves the other's disk-exhaustion risk entirely unaddressed.
6. **Differentiate retention policy by log category** (Chapter 4, Section 3.2) rather than applying one blanket period uniformly — security and audit data generally warrants longer retention than high-volume, low-diagnostic-value debug output.
7. **Prefer TCP over UDP for any syslog transport carrying data with genuine operational or evidentiary value** (Chapter 3, Section 4; Chapter 9, Section 3.1) — the reliability gap is not a minor trade-off, and it compounds at fleet scale.
8. **Scope audit rules deliberately and narrowly around genuinely security-relevant events** (Chapter 7, Section 3.1) rather than either leaving audit logging at its unconfigured baseline or attempting indiscriminate, blanket coverage.
9. **Choose audit failure behavior consciously, based on actual regulatory and threat-model context** (Chapter 7, Section 4.2) — `SUSPEND`/`halt` is a deliberate, sometimes-mandated choice, not a default to leave unexamined.
10. **Encrypt log-shipping transport for any centralized deployment** (Chapter 9, Section 6.1) — reliable delivery alone doesn't address interception or in-transit tampering risk.
11. **Treat the centralized log aggregation point itself as a high-value security target warranting elevated hardening priority** (Chapter 9, Section 6.2), directly applying the permissions series' own hardening methodology with particular rigor to this specific system.
12. **Monitor disk usage actively, on every host and at every layer (local storage, shipper queues, central storage), rather than relying on rotation configuration alone** (Chapter 4, Section 5; Chapter 9, Section 3.2).
13. **Document, for any non-obvious application, exactly where its logs actually go** (Chapter 6, Section 6) — sparing future diagnostic effort the `lsof`-based empirical investigation Chapter 6, Section 4.3 covers as a fallback.
14. **When correlating across sources, reconcile timestamp format and precision before trusting apparent chronological ordering** (Chapter 8, Section 3.3; Chapter 9, Section 4) — a genuinely common, easy-to-miss source of false conclusions.
15. **Recognize the boundary where log data alone is insufficient, and treat a recurring diagnostic gap as an instrumentation improvement opportunity** (Chapter 8, Section 6) rather than repeatedly re-querying an already-exhausted set of sources.

## 2. Worked Scenario One: The Vanishing Logs After a Routine Reboot

**Symptom:** Following a scheduled maintenance reboot, an administrator investigating a pre-reboot incident finds `journalctl -b -1` returns nothing at all — no data from the previous boot whatsoever, despite the incident having generated substantial log activity at the time.

**Diagnosis, following the series' cumulative material:** This is a direct, textbook instance of Chapter 2, Section 2's volatile-versus-persistent distinction. Checking `Storage=` in `/etc/systemd/journald.conf` reveals no explicit setting, meaning the default `auto` behavior applies — and checking `/var/log/journal/` reveals it does not exist on this specific system. Per Chapter 2, Section 2.2's precise mechanism, journald therefore fell back to volatile, `tmpfs`-backed storage under `/run/log/journal/` for the entirety of the previous boot's uptime — and, per Section 1.3, tmpfs is memory-backed and genuinely, unrecoverably cleared on reboot, meaning the previous boot's entire journal history is not merely hard to find; it is actually gone, with no recovery technique this series has covered (or any technique at all) able to retrieve it after the fact.

**Fix, and the more important lesson:** `mkdir -p /var/log/journal && systemd-tmpfiles --create --prefix /var/log/journal` ensures this doesn't recur — but the actual, more consequential lesson is procedural, not technical: this is precisely the kind of consequential, easily-overlooked default this chapter's Section 1, item 2 flags explicitly as warranting deliberate, documented decision-making at initial system provisioning time, rather than being discovered, after the fact, at exactly the moment persistent historical data would have been most valuable — a pattern worth generalizing well beyond this specific scenario: several of this series' most operationally consequential facts (this one, Chapter 4's rotation-versus-retention split, Chapter 7's audit failure-behavior choice) share the property of being invisible, low-consequence defaults right up until the specific moment a real incident makes their absence suddenly, sharply consequential.

## 3. Worked Scenario Two: A Compliance Audit Reveals an Incomplete Audit Trail

**Symptom:** During a routine compliance review, an auditor requests a complete record of every modification to `/etc/passwd` and `/etc/shadow` over the preceding six months. `ausearch -k identity-changes --start "6 months ago"` returns results covering only the most recent three weeks.

**Diagnosis:** This connects two separate chapters' material directly. First, confirming the audit rule itself (Chapter 7, Section 3.2) is correctly configured and has been in place — it has, and has been generating events correctly. The actual gap is therefore not a rule-configuration problem but a **retention** problem, directly per Chapter 4's material applied specifically to audit data: checking `auditd`'s own log-rotation configuration (`/etc/audit/auditd.conf`) reveals a `max_log_file` and `num_logs` setting that, multiplied together, produces a total retention window of only about three weeks under the system's actual observed audit-event volume — consistent, precisely, with the missing five-plus months of the requested compliance window.

**Fix:** Adjusting `auditd`'s own rotation parameters to a retention window that genuinely satisfies the compliance requirement, and — directly per Chapter 9, Section 5.3's specific recommendation — considering whether this specific data category warrants forwarding to centralized, potentially longer-retained storage rather than relying exclusively on local, single-host retention for data with a genuine, externally-mandated minimum retention requirement at all.

**Lesson:** This scenario is a direct, worked illustration of Chapter 7, Section 1.3's framing taken to its real, practical consequence — audit logging's entire value proposition rests on reliable, sufficient retention meeting whatever external requirement actually applies, and a technically correctly-configured audit *rule* (Chapter 7, Section 3) that nonetheless sits behind an inadequately configured *retention* window (this chapter's own subject, Chapter 4) produces exactly this outcome: audit events were genuinely, correctly generated at the time they occurred, and are just as genuinely, irrecoverably gone by the time they're actually needed, precisely because rule configuration and retention configuration are two entirely separate concerns, each requiring its own deliberate attention, exactly as Chapter 4's own opening material warned.

## 4. Worked Scenario Three: An Incident That Spans Multiple Hosts and Multiple Logging Mechanisms

**Symptom:** A production incident — an intermittent, hard-to-reproduce API failure — is reported. The failure seems to correlate loosely with specific times of day but not with any single, identifiable backend host.

**Diagnosis, applying Chapter 8 and Chapter 9's combined methodology:** Following Chapter 8, Section 1.2's decision framework, this is explicitly a cross-source, cross-host correlation problem from the outset, not a single-mechanism or single-host lookup — directly calling for Chapter 9's centralized logging infrastructure rather than Chapter 8's single-host techniques alone. Querying the centralized aggregation point with a deliberately broad, unfiltered, time-bounded query (Chapter 8, Section 2.2's correlation-first pattern, now applied at fleet scale) across the approximate failure window surfaces a pattern invisible from any single host's own local logs: a specific backend instance, different each time the failure recurs, briefly shows elevated response latency in its own local journald output immediately preceding each reported failure — but critically, no error-level entry from that specific instance alone would have stood out as clearly anomalous in isolation, since each individual instance's own local logs show only a brief, easily-dismissed latency blip, not an outright failure.

Cross-referencing this pattern against the centralized system's own receipt-side timestamps (Chapter 9, Section 4.3) against the shipped, host-originated timestamps reveals the actual root cause: the affected instance, each time, is specifically the one that happens to be mid-rotation of its own local journal at that exact moment (Chapter 2, Section 4.2's per-boot and per-size rotation mechanism), and a resource-intensive rotation operation, occurring under specifically heavy concurrent request load, produces exactly the brief latency spike observed — a genuine, if narrow, interaction between Chapter 4's rotation mechanics and application performance, invisible to any single-host investigation precisely because no individual instance's own logs, viewed alone, showed anything unambiguously wrong.

**Fix:** Tuning the affected services' journald rotation thresholds (Chapter 2, Section 4.3) to trigger during lower-traffic windows where feasible, and, more generally, adding rotation-timing metadata to the standard set of signals monitored during incident investigation going forward.

**Lesson:** This scenario is deliberately constructed to require the full stack this series has built, in combination: Chapter 8's cross-mechanism methodology, Chapter 9's fleet-wide correlation infrastructure, and Chapter 4's rotation-mechanics material, brought together specifically because the actual root cause was invisible to any one of these three areas of knowledge applied in isolation — directly the kind of scenario this closing chapter, and its counterpart in the permissions series, exist specifically to model: real incidents rarely respect the chapter boundaries any structured reference material necessarily imposes, and genuine diagnostic skill lies in fluidly combining material from across an entire series' scope, not in mastering any single chapter's content in isolation.

## 5. Worked Scenario Four: A Well-Intentioned Security Hardening Change Breaks Legitimate Troubleshooting

**Symptom:** Following a security hardening initiative that enabled `auditd`'s `disk_full_action = halt` configuration (Chapter 7, Section 4.2) across the fleet, a routine, unrelated disk-space issue on a single host — not itself security-relevant, simply an underprovisioned `/var` partition gradually filling from ordinary, non-audit log growth — triggers an unexpected, full system halt.

**Diagnosis:** This is worth working through carefully because it's a genuinely subtle, realistic interaction between two separate chapters' material, rather than a simple misconfiguration in either individually. The `halt`-on-disk-full behavior was deliberately configured, correctly per Chapter 7, Section 4.2's own reasoning, specifically to guarantee audit-trail completeness — but the actual trigger, on investigation, turns out to be `/var/log/audit/` and general application logs (Chapter 6) sharing the *same underlying filesystem*, meaning ordinary, non-audit log growth from an entirely unrelated source was sufficient to exhaust the shared filesystem's available space, triggering `auditd`'s disk-full detection and its configured `halt` response — even though the audit log's own specific growth was entirely unremarkable and had nothing to do with the actual cause.

**Fix:** Placing `/var/log/audit/` on its own, dedicated filesystem or partition, separate from general application and operational logging — directly ensuring that ordinary, non-audit log volume (Chapter 4's entire subject) cannot, even indirectly, trigger audit-specific failure behavior (Chapter 7's subject) that was only ever intended to respond to genuine audit-data-specific storage exhaustion.

**Lesson:** This scenario illustrates a genuinely important, easy-to-overlook interaction this series' chapter-by-chapter structure could, if read too literally as fully independent topics, obscure: Chapter 4's general rotation/retention material and Chapter 7's audit-specific failure-behavior material are not actually independent in their real-world consequences whenever they share underlying infrastructure (here, a filesystem) — a deliberate, well-reasoned configuration choice in one domain (audit's `halt` behavior) can be inadvertently triggered by an entirely unrelated condition in the other domain (ordinary log growth) if the two aren't given appropriately isolated resources, a specific, concrete argument for the dedicated-partition recommendation this fix applies, and a broader, generalizable lesson about auditing not just individual configuration choices but the *shared infrastructure* those choices ultimately depend on.

## 6. Worked Scenario Five: Debugging a Silent Application That Appears to Log Nothing At All

**Symptom:** A newly deployed, custom internal service, managed as a `systemd` unit, appears to produce no log output whatsoever — `journalctl -u custom-service.service` returns entirely empty results, despite the service apparently running and, per external monitoring, apparently functioning correctly.

**Diagnosis, working through Chapter 6's full elimination sequence:** First, confirming the unit is genuinely running (`systemctl status custom-service.service`) — it is, ruling out the simplest possible explanation. Next, per Chapter 6, Section 2.2, checking the unit file's `StandardOutput=`/`StandardError=` directives directly — both are left at their default, unset value, meaning the `journal` default (Chapter 6, Section 2.1) should apply, seemingly ruling out an explicit redirect-away-from-journald misconfiguration as well.

The actual resolution requires Chapter 6, Section 4.3's empirical fallback technique: `lsof -p <pid>` against the running service's actual process reveals it has a specific, application-internal log file open directly — the application, despite being run under `systemd` with default output settings, was written to explicitly detect whether it's running interactively versus under a supervisor, and, upon detecting non-interactive (supervised) execution, deliberately opens and writes to its own hardcoded internal log path entirely on its own initiative, **bypassing stdout/stderr entirely** rather than relying on them — meaning `StandardOutput=journal`'s default capture (Chapter 6, Section 2.1) was never actually going to have anything to capture at all, since the application never wrote to stdout/stderr in the first place under this specific execution mode, an application-internal behavior invisible from the unit file's own configuration alone.

**Fix:** Either reconfiguring the application (if its own configuration supports it) to write to stdout/stderr instead of its internal hardcoded path, restoring the zero-effort journald integration Chapter 6, Section 6 recommends as best practice, or, if the application's own behavior can't be changed, explicitly documenting the actual, empirically-determined log location (directly per Chapter 6, Section 6's documentation recommendation) so future diagnostic effort doesn't need to re-derive this same finding through the same `lsof`-based investigation.

**Lesson:** This scenario is a direct, concrete illustration of Chapter 6, Section 4's core distinction between `systemd`-managed *process supervision* and actual *logging integration* — being run under `systemd` at all does not, by itself, guarantee an application's output reaches journald; it guarantees only that *if* the application writes to stdout/stderr, that output will be captured by default. An application that deliberately, internally chooses a different output path entirely sits outside this guarantee regardless of its supervision status, precisely the gap Chapter 6, Section 4.3's `lsof`-based technique exists specifically to close when documentation and unit-file inspection alone prove insufficient.

## 7. Closing Synthesis: The Throughline Across Both Series

This ten-chapter logging series, alongside its ten-chapter permissions predecessor, has followed a consistent methodological commitment worth naming explicitly as this entire project's closing note: **derive mechanism before stating rules.** Every chapter across both series has favored explaining *why* a given behavior exists — the boot-order constraint that necessitates the kernel ring buffer, the accountability requirement that necessitates a structurally separate audit UID, the specific reliability gap that makes UDP syslog transport a genuinely different risk category from TCP rather than merely a speed trade-off — over simply presenting each fact as an isolated, memorized rule.

This approach is what makes the worked scenarios in this closing chapter, and their direct counterparts in the permissions series' own final chapter, actually tractable: every one of the five scenarios above required combining material from at least two, and in Scenario Three's case, three or more, of the preceding nine chapters, in ways no single chapter's own material, read in isolation, could have anticipated or fully prepared for. That combinatorial flexibility — the ability to recognize which of a dozen previously-covered mechanisms is actually relevant to a novel, real situation, and to reason correctly about how those mechanisms interact — is precisely what mechanism-level understanding provides that rule-memorization alone does not, and it is the specific, deliberate capability both of these ten-chapter series have been built, from their respective opening chapters onward, to instill.
